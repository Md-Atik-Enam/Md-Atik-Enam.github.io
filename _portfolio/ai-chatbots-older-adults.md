  <section class="case-section case-section--tight">
    <h2>Problem</h2>
    <p>Generative AI products are moving into everyday life, but older adults are often treated as secondary users or framed through deficit-based assumptions. For product teams, this creates a risk: AI systems may be technically powerful yet fail on onboarding, credibility, privacy, and accessibility for a major user group.</p>
    <p>The research question was practical: after a first hands-on experience with an LLM chatbot, what makes older adults see it as useful, trustworthy, risky, or worth adopting?</p>
  </section>

  <section class="case-section">
    <h2>Research Design</h2>
    <div class="case-timeline" aria-label="Research process">
      <div><strong>1. Baseline</strong><span>Captured demographics and AI literacy before the product interaction.</span></div>
      <div><strong>2. Guided demo</strong><span>Introduced ChatGPT 3.5 with neutral example prompts to reduce interface friction.</span></div>
      <div><strong>3. Open exploration</strong><span>Gave participants 15-25 minutes to ask their own questions and form first impressions.</span></div>
      <div><strong>4. Interview</strong><span>Ran 25-45 minute semi-structured interviews on usability, trust, health, companionship, privacy, and future uses.</span></div>
    </div>
    <p>Across the study, we collected more than 18 hours of audio. The analysis moved from 38 initial codes to a refined 27-code codebook, then into four higher-level themes that translate directly into product strategy.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="case-section">
    <h2>Method Choice</h2>
    <div class="case-method-grid">
      <div>
        <h3>Alternative: Survey</h3>
        <p>A survey could have measured attitudes at scale, but it would have missed how participants formed opinions while actually trying the chatbot, especially when confusion, delight, privacy concern, or credibility checking happened in the moment.</p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3>Alternative: Task-based usability test</h3>
        <p>A narrow usability test could have identified interface breakdowns, but the main question was broader: how older adults make sense of an unfamiliar AI system, what they trust it with, and where they see value in daily life.</p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3>Alternative: Longitudinal diary study</h3>
        <p>A diary study could show adoption over time, but it would require participants to already know enough to use the tool independently. That would bias the study away from first impressions and early onboarding barriers.</p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3>Chosen: Contextual interviews</h3>
        <p>Contextual interviews let me observe first-use behavior, capture self-directed prompts, and immediately probe the reasoning behind trust, usefulness, privacy boundaries, health-information expectations, and social-companion reactions.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="case-section">
    <h2>Representative Data</h2>
    <div class="case-evidence-grid">
      <div class="case-stat"><strong>20</strong><span>older adults participated, ages 59-93, with an average age of 73.2.</span></div>
      <div class="case-stat"><strong>18+ hrs</strong><span>of audio data were collected and analyzed through reflexive thematic analysis.</span></div>
      <div class="case-stat"><strong>43.88%</strong><span>was the mean self-assessed AI literacy score, creating a realistic novice-user context.</span></div>
      <div class="case-stat"><strong>15 of 20</strong><span>responded positively to chatbots as potential social companions, though mostly with limits.</span></div>
    </div>
    <div class="case-quote-grid">

  <section class="case-section">
    <h2>Key Insights</h2>
    <div class="case-insight-grid">
      <article>
        <span class="case-tag">Mental models</span>
        <h3>Participants saw the chatbot as an expert, a neutral diplomat, or a potential companion.</h3>
        <p>These mental models shaped whether they expected the system to provide facts, balanced perspectives, emotional support, or expert advice.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span class="case-tag case-tag--amber">Trust</span>
        <h3>Fast, personal answers were compelling, but source credibility was a deciding factor.</h3>
        <p>Participants appreciated responses that felt tailored, but wanted recognizable sources, especially for unfamiliar or higher-stakes topics.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span class="case-tag case-tag--rose">Risk</span>
        <h3>Privacy uncertainty restricted what people were willing to share.</h3>
        <p>When participants did not know how prompts were stored or used, they avoided personal details that would have made the chatbot more helpful.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span class="case-tag case-tag--blue">Accessibility</span>
        <h3>Voice and multimodal responses matter, but the format should match the task.</h3>
        <p>Voice could make interaction easier, while visual aids, videos, or printable summaries may be better for instructions that users need to revisit.</p>
      </article>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="case-section">
    <h2>What This Means for Product Teams</h2>
    <div class="case-recommendations">
      <div>
        <h3>Design transparent trust cues</h3>
        <p>Show where information comes from, explain confidence and limitations, and distinguish general information from advice that requires expert verification.</p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3>Make onboarding visible and low-friction</h3>
        <p>Provide prompt examples, clearer send affordances, and first-run guidance designed for users who may not already know AI interaction patterns.</p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3>Offer privacy controls in plain language</h3>
        <p>Explain what is stored, who can access it, whether data trains models, and how users can keep conversations private.</p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3>Use task-aware multimodality</h3>
        <p>Pair speech with text, summaries, videos, or step-by-step artifacts depending on whether the user is exploring, deciding, remembering, or acting.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
